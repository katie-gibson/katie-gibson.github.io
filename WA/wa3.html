Michelin Guide

The Michelin Guides are a series of guide books published for exceptionally rated restaurants in certain areas and regions.

History

In 1900, there were fewer than 3000 cars on the road in France. To increase the demand for cars and subsequently car tires, two tire manufacturers created a guide for French motorists. The first 35,000 copies were free and provided information like hotels, mechanics, maps, and tire repair.

After the publication was suspended in WW1, the guides were revised and they started to be sold for $2.15 in 1922. In 1926, the guides started to include fine dining establishments and 10 years later the criteria for the rankings were published:

  • ☆ "a very good restaurant in its category"
  • ☆☆ "excellent cooking, worth a detour"
  • ☆☆☆ "exceptional cuisine, worth a special journey"

    • The guide paused again during WW2, and after the war expanded into several more countries covering hotels and restaurants all of the world.

    Methods & Layout

    Stars

    Michelin reviwers visit restaurants anonymously, usually in pairs, and they award one, two, or three stars accordingly. See above under History for the rating criteria. The guide also awards "Rising Stars" for restaurants that have the potential to earn stars.

    Green Stars

    In 2020, Michelin launched "Green Stars" which are a sustainability emblem. These symbolize excellence in sustainable gastronomy, and those awarded with this are given space to describe their restaurant vision.

    Bib Gourmand

    Since 1997, the guide has highlighted restaurants offering "exceptionally good food at moderate prices," which is called "Bib Gourmand."

    The Plate

    In 2016, "The Plate," was added to recognize restaurants that "simply serve good food."
